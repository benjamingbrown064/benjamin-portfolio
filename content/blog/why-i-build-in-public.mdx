There's a question I get asked often: why do you document everything you're building in real time?

The cynical answer is that it's good marketing. The honest answer is that it's the only way I know how to work that actually holds me accountable.

When I started building One Beyond, I kept everything private. The plans, the failures, the pivots. It felt safer that way. You can't be criticised for decisions no one knows you've made. You can quietly walk back a bad call without having to explain yourself.

But quiet also means invisible. And invisible means no feedback loop.

## What Building in Public Actually Means

Building in public doesn't mean sharing your pitch deck on Twitter or posting motivational updates about your "journey." That's just marketing with extra steps.

Real building in public means:

- Sharing the decision you're unsure about before you've made it
- Documenting the thing that didn't work and why
- Publishing your reasoning, not just your results
- Being on the record about what you're trying to achieve

When you do those things, you get something you can't buy: honest feedback from people who have no reason to be polite.

## The Accountability Loop

Here's what I've noticed after building openly for two years: the act of documenting forces you to think more clearly.

You can hold a vague idea in your head for weeks without ever pressure-testing it. The moment you try to write it down clearly enough for someone else to understand, you find the holes. Every time.

I've killed more bad ideas by trying to write them up than by any formal review process. The discipline of explaining yourself is a discipline in itself.

## It Attracts the Right People

The best hires, the best clients, and the best partners I've worked with all came from seeing the work publicly. Not a polished case study — the actual process. The rough edges. The thinking that went into decisions.

When someone reaches out because they've watched you build something and they understand how you think, you skip about six months of trust-building. They already know if they want to work with you.

The people who are put off by transparency weren't going to be good fits anyway.

## The Hard Part

The hard part is posting the failure.

It's easy to share when things are going well. The shipped product, the happy customer, the good metric. But the value of building in public comes mostly from sharing the other stuff.

The launch that went quiet. The feature that nobody used. The decision that seemed obvious at the time and cost you three weeks to unpick.

Those posts are uncomfortable to write. They're also the ones that generate the best conversations, attract the most useful people, and teach you the most about your own thinking.

## A Practical Note

If you're a founder and you're not building in public because you're worried about competitors seeing your work: they're not watching. They're too busy with their own problems.

And if someone does copy what you're doing, they'll copy the output, not the thinking. The thinking is the unfair advantage, and that's exactly what you're developing by documenting it.

Start with one honest post. Not about your vision. About a real decision you made this week and why. See what happens.

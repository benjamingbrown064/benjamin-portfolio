Most technology projects fail not because of technical problems.

They fail because of misalignment between what was built and what was needed. That misalignment is usually visible long before it becomes a write-off — but only if you're looking for it in the right way.

The Discovery Programme is a two-week structured engagement that exists to find that misalignment before it costs six months of development.

## The Problem It Solves

When a business comes to me with a technology project, the conversation usually starts with a solution. "We need a portal for our customers." "We need an app for our field team." "We need to integrate our CRM with our billing system."

Solutions are hard to evaluate without understanding the problem they're solving, the context they need to work in, and the constraints on what good looks like.

Jumping straight from "we need X" to "let's build X" skips the most important work: understanding whether X is the right thing to build, whether this is the right time to build it, and whether the business is set up to get value from it once it exists.

## What Happens in Two Weeks

**Week one is entirely research.** Structured interviews with the people who will use the system and the people who will be accountable for it. They are not the same people, and they often have fundamentally different views on what success looks like.

I ask about current workflows, not imagined ones. I ask about what breaks, not what could be better. I ask about what happens when the system doesn't work, because that tells you more about the actual dependencies than any description of the intended workflow.

I also map the technical landscape — what systems exist, where the data lives, what integration constraints are real versus assumed.

**Week two is synthesis and specification.** Taking the research and translating it into a clear product specification: what the system needs to do, what it doesn't need to do, what the architecture should look like, what the risks are, and what a realistic build timeline and cost looks like.

The output is a document, not a prototype. Prototypes answer questions about UX. At this stage, the important questions are about whether we should build at all, and if so, what exactly.

## What It Typically Finds

Every Discovery Programme finds something unexpected. That's its value.

Common findings:

**The problem is smaller than described.** The "portal" needs four features, not forty. The integration is one API call, not a full middleware layer. Scoping down dramatically reduces cost and risk.

**The problem is different from the presented problem.** The field team doesn't need a mobile app. They need a clearer process for what happens after they finish a job. Technology is a band-aid on a workflow problem.

**There's a dependency that wasn't mentioned.** The system can't be built until the data is clean, until a vendor provides an API, until a process is standardised. Building now would mean rebuilding later.

**The stakeholders disagree on what success looks like.** This one is critical. If the people accountable for the system and the people who will use it have different definitions of success, no product will satisfy both. That conversation has to happen before any code is written.

## Why Two Weeks

Two weeks is long enough to do real research and short enough to keep the cost of discovery proportionate to the cost of the project.

The Discovery Programme typically costs between 5% and 10% of the total project cost. In most cases, it saves between 20% and 100% of that cost — either by scoping down what needs to be built, by preventing a project that shouldn't be built, or by identifying a dependency that would have caused a late-stage rebuild.

For anyone considering a technology project over £50,000: the discovery is not optional. It's the most important two weeks in the project.

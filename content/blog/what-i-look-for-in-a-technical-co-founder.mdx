The technical co-founder question is one of the most consequential decisions a non-technical founder makes.

Get it right and you have a partner who makes everything you're trying to build faster, more resilient, and better quality. Get it wrong and you have a dependency that becomes a liability — either because the person isn't right, or because the structure of the relationship doesn't work.

Most people get it wrong in the same way.

## The Wrong Filter

The most common filter non-technical founders use: "Can this person build what I've described?"

This is the wrong question. Almost any competent engineer can build what you've described in the early stage. The hard part isn't building it — it's deciding what to build, in what order, with what architecture, and what to drop when you're wrong about something.

The right filter: "Does this person think clearly about problems I don't understand, and can I evaluate the quality of their thinking even when I can't evaluate the output?"

## What Good Technical Thinking Looks Like

You don't need to be technical to spot good technical thinking. You just need to know what to listen for.

Good technical thinkers:
- Start with constraints, not solutions
- Are more interested in the problem than in demonstrating their knowledge
- Give you a range of options with trade-offs, not a single "correct" answer
- Say "I don't know" cleanly rather than speculating fluently
- Push back on requirements that don't make sense

Poor technical thinkers:
- Start with the technology they want to use
- Name-drop frameworks and tools before understanding the problem
- Treat complexity as a sign of quality
- Can't explain what they built to a non-technical audience
- Say yes to everything in the early conversations

## The Equity Conversation

This is where most early co-founder relationships fail.

If someone is truly a co-founder — if they're taking real risk, investing real time, making real decisions — the equity split should reflect that. 50/50 for two co-founders, 33/33/33 for three, adjusted for timing and relative contribution.

If someone is joining after the company is established, has a salary, and isn't betting their livelihood on the outcome — they're an early employee, not a co-founder. Treating them as a co-founder in title to attract them is a shortcut that creates problems later.

The question of what constitutes "real risk" is worth being precise about. Real risk means: this person's financial situation is meaningfully affected by whether this works. If it isn't, the alignment is wrong, regardless of what the cap table says.

## The Working Style Question

Technical execution is only one part of the role. The other part is communication — with you, with the rest of the team, with customers.

A technical co-founder who can't explain trade-offs clearly, who gets defensive about technical debt, who sees product decisions as encroachment on their domain — these are predictors of future conflict.

The working style conversation should happen before any agreement is reached. Ask about a project that went wrong. Ask about a technical decision they later regretted. Ask about a time they disagreed with a product decision and how they handled it.

The answers are more valuable than any coding exercise.

## The Thing Most People Skip

The reference check.

Not a professional reference — those are performative. A reference from someone who worked with them closely on something that didn't go perfectly.

Ask that person: "What was the hardest moment working with them and how did they behave?" Ask: "If you were starting a company, would you take them as a technical co-founder?" Ask: "Is there anything you'd want me to know that I wouldn't find out from talking to them directly?"

You learn almost nothing from good references except that the person has worked with people who are willing to say good things. You learn the important things from the questions that require a considered answer.

## A Final Note

If you're a non-technical founder who is technical enough to evaluate code — be careful. Technical fluency can make you more likely to optimise for the wrong thing: someone whose code you can read and understand, rather than someone whose thinking is better than yours.

You want a technical co-founder who is substantially stronger than you in their domain. Not someone whose decisions you can second-guess.

The best technical partnership is one where you genuinely don't understand how they did it, and you trust that it's correct anyway.

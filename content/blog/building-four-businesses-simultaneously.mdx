I currently run four independent technology businesses. Not as a portfolio play. Not because I planned it this way. As an experiment in understanding where the real constraints on growth actually sit.

Here's what I've learned.

## The Focus Orthodoxy Is Wrong (Mostly)

Startup orthodoxy says: focus on one thing. Don't get distracted. Say no to everything that isn't your primary objective.

This advice is correct for most people and wrong for some. The question is which one you are.

The advice is correct when the limiting factor is your decision-making capacity. If you're at the early stage where every important decision requires your direct involvement, spreading across multiple businesses reduces quality across all of them. This is the common case.

The advice breaks down when you've built systems that can absorb many of your routine decisions. When the systems are doing the management, your attention can move faster across contexts than most people assume.

I'm not recommending running four businesses. I'm saying the constraint isn't always focus. Sometimes it's systems.

## What Actually Gets Complicated

The real complications of running multiple businesses are not what the focus orthodoxy predicts.

**Cash flow complexity.** When you have four P&Ls, the timing of revenue and expense across all of them requires more active management than a single business. A slow month in one can be masked by a strong month in another, which creates a false sense of stability.

**Talent allocation.** The right person for a problem in one business is often the right person for a similar problem in another. When they're the same person you are, this is fine. When you're building teams, you have to decide whether people work across contexts or own a single domain. The answer varies.

**Strategic drift.** Each business has its own logical next move. If you pursue all of them simultaneously, you end up with four businesses each at 70% of their potential rather than one business at full throttle. This is a real cost.

**Context switching overhead.** Moving between fundamentally different businesses requires a gear change that takes time. The first 20 minutes after switching context are lower quality. If you switch four times a day, that's over an hour of degraded thinking.

## What Actually Gets Easier

**Pattern recognition.** Problems that look unique in a single business context reveal themselves as common patterns when you see them across multiple contexts. Pricing psychology, churn drivers, team dynamic failures — they look the same across industries once you've seen them enough times.

**Cross-pollination.** An approach that works well in one business is often directly applicable in another. Operational systems, customer communication frameworks, technical architecture decisions — these transfer.

**Risk tolerance.** When you have multiple income streams, the pressure on any single business decision decreases. You can afford to run an experiment, take a calculated risk, or wait for the right moment rather than forcing a move because the single business needs to grow at all costs.

## The Actual Model

I don't treat all four businesses as equal priorities. At any given point, one is in active growth mode, one is in maintenance mode, one is in build mode, and one is in validation mode.

The operating intensity required for each is very different. A business in maintenance mode requires attention only when something is wrong. A business in active growth mode requires daily focus.

The rotation matters. Nothing stays in growth mode indefinitely without burning out the people running it. And nothing stays in maintenance mode without eventually declining.

## What I'd Tell Myself Earlier

Don't conflate "I can do this" with "I should do this."

The intellectual challenge of managing multiple businesses is genuinely stimulating. It's also a form of avoidance. It's easier to start a new thing than to do the hard, slow work of taking an existing thing from good to great.

I've built a lot. I've taken fewer things as far as they could have gone because I moved on to the next build before fully exploring the current one.

That's the real lesson. Not whether you can run multiple businesses. Whether you're running multiple businesses instead of going deeper on one.

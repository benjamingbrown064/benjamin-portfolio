Every week there's another post about how AI is going to make developers obsolete. I've been shipping software with AI tools for two years now, and I have a different read on it.

AI doesn't replace builders. It raises the floor. And raising the floor changes who wins.

## What AI Actually Does

Let me be precise about what AI coding tools do well, because "AI writes code" is a useless description.

AI is very good at:
- Producing syntactically correct code for common patterns
- Translating requirements into boilerplate
- Explaining what a piece of code does
- Finding and fixing syntax errors
- Writing tests for functions you describe clearly

AI is genuinely poor at:
- Understanding why a product decision was made
- Knowing when a technically correct solution is architecturally wrong
- Evaluating trade-offs that require business context
- Debugging problems caused by incorrect assumptions in the original spec
- Knowing when to say no to a requirement

The gap between "generates code" and "ships good products" is exactly where the domain knowledge lives.

## The Floor Goes Up, the Ceiling Stays

Anyone who can write a clear prompt can now produce a working UI component in 30 seconds. That's genuinely remarkable. It means the floor of what non-technical people can create has risen dramatically.

But the ceiling — the quality of a properly thought-through system, a coherent architecture, a product that scales without becoming a liability — that ceiling is still determined by the quality of the builder's thinking.

I've used AI to ship faster on every project I've built in the last two years. I've also watched projects built almost entirely by AI prompting fall apart the moment they encountered real-world complexity. The code was there. The understanding wasn't.

## Domain Knowledge Is the Unfair Advantage

When I build a warranty management platform, I'm making dozens of micro-decisions that don't appear in any prompt. How claims should be structured so they're auditable. Where the liability sits when a decision is made without complete information. What the dealer's workflow actually looks like at 8pm on a Saturday.

That knowledge comes from talking to the people who do the work. It comes from building the first version badly and understanding why. It doesn't come from a model trained on Stack Overflow.

The builders who win over the next decade aren't the ones who can prompt most fluently. They're the ones who know their domain deeply enough to know what good looks like — and can use AI to get there faster.

## What You Should Actually Do

If you're a builder: learn how to use the tools properly. The productivity gain is real. But invest the time you save into going deeper on the domain problems, not shallower on the craft.

If you're a founder looking to hire: the question isn't "can they use AI?" It's "do they know when the AI output is wrong?" That's the skill that's actually scarce.

If you're worried about being replaced: you're probably not. But the version of you that doesn't develop real domain expertise — that version is at risk.

The tools change. The requirement for genuine understanding doesn't.

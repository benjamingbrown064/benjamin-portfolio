Most SaaS products that fail never make it to the failure mode people discuss — wrong pricing, slow growth, churn.

They fail earlier, and quieter. The founders often don't realise it until they've already spent six months building something nobody needed.

Here are the patterns I've seen most often.

## Building a Solution Before Understanding the Problem

The most common failure is also the most avoidable.

A founder notices something inconvenient in their own workflow. They assume others share the problem. They build a solution. They launch. Crickets.

The problem isn't that their workflow observation was wrong. It's that they confused personal friction with market demand.

Personal friction means: this thing bothers me.
Market demand means: enough people have this problem, feel it acutely enough to pay to solve it, and have no acceptable alternative.

Those aren't the same thing. And you can't determine the gap by thinking harder about it. You have to talk to the people who supposedly have the problem and ask them what they currently do about it.

If they say "I just deal with it" or "we use a spreadsheet" — that's not validation. That's a sign the friction isn't acute enough to pay for.

## Solving the Symptom, Not the Problem

The second failure mode: building a tool that addresses what customers complain about, not what's actually making them unhappy.

Customers are good at describing their pain and poor at describing its cause. "Our onboarding process is slow" is a symptom. The cause might be unclear ownership, missing documentation, wrong tooling, or an approval chain that creates bottlenecks.

If you build a faster onboarding tool without understanding why onboarding is slow, you might produce something that improves the metric while leaving the underlying problem unchanged.

This is why discovery conversations are structured around behaviour, not opinions. "Walk me through the last time you did X" is more useful than "what would make X better?"

## Building for Edge Cases First

Early-stage products are usually over-engineered for scenarios that happen infrequently.

The team imagines their product in the hands of a power user with complex requirements. They build the edge case handling, the configurable workflows, the admin panel with seventeen options.

The common case — which represents 80% of actual usage — is handled adequately but not well. The product feels bloated because it is.

Start with the common case. Make it excellent. Add edge case handling when real customers actually encounter the edge case.

## Pricing That Signals the Wrong Thing

Pricing too low is a failure mode most founders don't anticipate.

If you price a B2B SaaS at £20/month because you're worried about objections, you create a different problem: the buyers you want — the ones with budgets, the ones who can make decisions quickly, the ones who don't churn after 30 days — don't take you seriously.

Price signals quality and commitment. A product at £20/month attracts a particular kind of customer. That customer has very different support expectations, very different churn behaviour, and very different expansion potential than a customer at £200/month.

Start by understanding the value your product creates for the customer. Price relative to that value, not relative to your anxiety about objections.

## The Pre-Launch Checklist That Actually Matters

Before you ship:

1. Have you spoken to at least 20 potential customers about the problem — not the product?
2. Have you watched 5 of them try to use your product without you explaining it?
3. Do you have at least 3 people who've said "I would pay for this" and followed it up with a payment?
4. Have you defined what "success in 90 days" looks like in concrete, measurable terms?
5. Do you know who specifically you're selling to first and why?

If you can't answer all five clearly, you're not ready to launch. You're ready to do more discovery.

The goal of a launch isn't to ship code. It's to start learning from real customers in real conditions. You can only do that if you've earned the right to their attention by understanding their problem first.
